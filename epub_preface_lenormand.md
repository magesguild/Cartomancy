## Between Chapters Three and Four

The English playing card system reads individual cards in defined positional spreads.
Each card makes a statement; the position tells you what domain the statement applies
to; surrounding cards modify it. The unit of meaning is the card.

The Lenormand system shares the same physical substrate — a standard playing card deck
underlies it — but uses it in an entirely different way. The unit of meaning is the
combination. A single Lenormand card gives you a noun with almost nothing attached.
Two cards give you a sentence. The Grand Tableau gives you a map of an entire life
read simultaneously.

A reader trained in the English system will recognize the playing card identities on
the Lenormand cards and find, at several points, direct conflicts between the oracle
image and the card's English cartomantic meaning. Those conflicts are documented in the
chapter that follows. They are not errors — they are the record of two independent
traditions that developed in parallel from the same physical object without reference
to each other.

The Hermes Playing Card Oracle by Robert M. Place, the specific deck addressed in the
second half of the chapter, makes these conflicts visible and literal: it places the
playing card identity primary and the oracle image secondary, which is historically
accurate to the Lenormand tradition and practically significant for any reader who
brings an existing playing card practice to it.
